On Investing

Public Markets

where I started

The framework I built up was top-down through the value chain. You start with the industry and figure out where the company is positioned in it, who has pricing power, what supply and demand look like, and where margin actually pools when you trace the upstream and downstream effects. That map is the first thing, before anything else about the company itself.

From there you go into the business. How it makes money, how viable the model is, what the management looks like, and whether the moat is real and durable. Margins matter, but they come later in the analysis.

Once you actually understand the company, you turn around and look outward. What does the sell side think, what metric or result is the market trading on, and is there some variant perception you have that isn't priced in. Alpha comes from that gap, and if it holds up to scrutiny, you take the bet.

I liked the investigative side of this work, the rabbit-holing into a company until I felt I understood it. What I didn't like was that playing quarter to quarter against consensus made me feel like the lever I was pulling was small. The work was good, but the outcome was largely out of my hands.

Private Equity

where I went next

That was what pulled me toward PE. I did a stint at Riverside, got on some interesting deals, and realized I liked looking at companies more than I liked modeling them.

My favorite era of investing in history is the LBO period of the 80s and 90s. The amount of operational control a sponsor could exert on outcomes back then is remarkable to study, and the levers you could actually pull on a business once you owned it were real. It was financial engineering paired with operational reshaping, in a window when both were genuinely underexploited.

That arbitrage is mostly closed now. Modern PE is operationally focused, the obvious financial moves are priced in, and a 20% IRR is no longer the baseline anyone underwrites to. The asset class still works, but the structural inefficiency that defined the LBO era doesn't exist at the same scale.

Venture

where I am now, thesis still forming

I'm still working on my thesis. What follows is a set of patterns I've noticed as I've been formulating it, and the framing I find most useful so far.

There are three parts to being a venture investor: finding the deal, winning the deal, and creating growth levers post-investment. I've spent the most time on the first.

Finding the deal

I think about this in tiers of causation. The founder is the impetus for everything, and industry and product flow downstream from there.

Founder. Three things I look for:

  1. The ability to sell a narrative and fundraise. The job of an early-stage founder is partially storytelling, to investors, to customers, and to recruits.
  2. The ability to acquire great talent. This requires real discernment, plus the ability to convince other talented people of the dream. Both are hard to fake.
  3. The ability to manage, or the self-awareness to hire people who can. Most technical founders don't have management instincts innately. The ones who figure it out, or who know they can't and hire around it, are the ones who scale.

Industry and product. Even the most brilliant founder won't generate venture returns in an area shielded from tailwinds. What I've noticed is that funds position themselves at different points along a tailwind, and there are roughly four positions to take.

  1. Downstream of where the tailwind takes us. Later-stage growth and crossover funds tend to operate here. They wait until the direction is clear and then bet on the winners that emerge.
  2. Where the tailwind itself comes from. Striker does a good amount of this, betting on the underlying driver rather than the visible end-state.
  3. What the tailwind enables. Most funds operate in this layer. The iPhone enabling Uber and Lyft is the canonical example. These are the most linear bets and the easiest to underwrite.
  4. What enables the tailwind itself. Picks and shovels. Red Glass investing in foundry robotics fits here, betting on the things that help shift the paradigm from x to y. These are shorter-duration bets, because once the enabling tech commoditizes the margin compresses. Myopic in theory, but in practice it tends to work during the window.

Product. The questions I work through:

  • Perception. How are end users responding to it, who are the real competitors, what does supply and demand look like, and is it viable in the long term.
  • Pivot potential. It's better to bet on an exceptional founder who will pivot (Cursor, Mercor) than to pass on something for not being perfect on the first iteration.
  • Monetization and the path to profitability.
  • If the idea isn't great, the question becomes how the founder responds to pressure on the metrics. How malleable are they to feedback, and how tied are they to the problem versus the specific solution they've landed on.
  • If the problem itself is wrong, the question is whether the engineering talent can pivot. The best case is a great problem and a great team, but a great team working on the wrong problem is salvageable.
  • Primary research. Try the product. Find where it falls apart. Look for edge cases that the founder hasn't thought through.
